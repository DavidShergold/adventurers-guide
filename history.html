The Creation of Dornat

Countless aeons before recorded history, the great high-god Helistarian created the universe and countless worlds within it. At some point he created a world he named Dornat. No one knows what this word meant as it is in the language of the gods. After creating this world he then created many gods lesser than him to act as caretakers over his creation. At first, these gods chose to walk the surface of the world, turning it into their own paradise bringing into existence great oceans, forests, deserts and tundra. Eventually the gods tired of the material realm ad instead, retreated to the heavens and instead created mortal life on Dornat.

The first life to appear took the form of animals, birds and fish. Next came the dragons and eventually creatures resembling humanoids that the gods named Samorians. For a time, everything was good but as the gods gave more and more bountiful gifts on their chosen children, large disasters started occurring. Huge earthquakes, volcanic eruptions and tidal waves struck at random across Dornat. The gods realised that they had upset the delicate balance that held the world together.

The gods separated themselves into two separate factions. Those who embraced creation, building and moving forward and those dedicated to destruction and decadence. These two groups realised that the prayers of the mortals they had created strengthened them began to actively influence the mortals into praying to their faction by offering them blessings and rewards in exchange for their worship. During this time, the Samorians, being the most easily influenced of the mortals, divided. Some into the light and some went to the darkness and over time, they began to change physically. Thus, elves and orc-kin came into the world.

Sadly, this golden age was not to last. As the gods flaunted their powers in order to gain followers, the very planet began to show the strain being put upon it. A few of the gods from both factions realised what was happening and took measures. They formed a third faction. One that existed in the grey and was dedicated to preserving the balance in all things. They persuaded the other factions to meet together and organised a set of rules that all three groups must abide by. Firstly, none of the gods was allowed to interfere directly with the mortal races as their power was too much for the planet to contain. Secondly, they were not permitted to walk the surface of the world physically. Lastly, each god was to choose a particular domain to rule over, nature, the ocean, love, and death etcetera. The mortals facing these things in life would then pray to that god for aide, thus, strengthening that god.

For the most part, the three pantheons kept to these new rules although they looked for loop holes whenever they could. They still appeared to mortals, although as spirits or in dreams instead of in person and used this new influence to guide their followers on the sly. A few times, they even guided them into situations that forced physical changes on them. Dwarves, gnomes, fairies, ogres, goblins and many more races came into being, some thriving and some remaining sparse.

The gods continue their power struggle and the races of mortals continue to do what is needed in order to survive life on Dornat.

The Three Golden Ages of Dornat

There were three ages in the long history of Dornat that have been termed “golden ages”. These three periods that lasted, at the shortest, two hundred years, were ages where mortals reached the pinnacle of culture through one means or the other. During these times, there was no war. Little to no famine or plague and everyone had plenty.

The First Golden Age

The Age of Perfection

This was considered, by most scholars to be the absolute apex of society that the peoples of Dornat have ever reached. At this stage, the gods had not long (by their view of time) been separated into separate pantheons and still freely mingled with their rival deities. The feuds and petty squabbles that would later cause such rifts between them had not been formed or been spilled into the minds of the mortal races.

As such, the mortals were able to flourish both technologically and spiritually. It was not uncommon to see temples dedicated to the gods of good to be built along side those to the gods of darkness and, for their priests and priestesses to get along as good friends. A man could walk into a temple of Horshan, the god of plague, without being considered an evil man. It was not uncommon for followers of justice and peace to pray to the dark gods, showing them reverence in the hope they would not unleash their evils upon them. Generally, the people of the world, uninfluenced by the gods petty rivalries, did not see the praying to of certain gods to be taboo and, the world was a better place for it.

Spell-casting was still a fresh concept to the mortal races and, manipulating the complex weaves of magic was still highly unexplored. However the discovery that magical power could stored inside precious stones and drawn off later (in much the same way that modern mages use their staffs) led to further exciting concepts. Many machines and contraptions had been designed by those with clever enough minds to do so in prior years, but had been abandoned due to a lack of power to fuel them. These “dragon-stones” as they became known, due to their volatile nature, were found to be able to supply these machines with the power they needed.

These strange and unusual pieces of technology thrust the people of Dornat even more firmly into a golden age. Great ships flew through the air, carrying people the length and breadth of the globe. The Great Continent and its surrounding islands became mapped for the first time in perfect detail. This machine also allowed trade throughout Dornat and allowed differing cultures to learn about each other. New weaponry and tools were invented. Ones that needed little skill to operate and enabled food growth and hunting to become far easier, ensuring that poverty became a thing of the past.

The very architecture of the cities of Dornat becomes more artistic. Slim yet tall building scrape the skies, linked to one another by vast walkways, high above the surface of the world. War becomes a thing of the past as people, enlightened by the religious and high-tech advancements around them. Sadly, this proved to be the ages downfall. When an elven mage discovered the concept of necromancy and tried to turn Dornat into their own play ground, there was very few who knew how to fight back.

The First Golden Age fell. Its technological marvels and religious enlightenment quickly turning to dust and becoming a story of legend. Nothing more than an idealists dream of what mortal-kind can achieve.

The Second Golden Age

The Age of Simplicity

If the First Golden Age had been the very heights to which mortal imagination could soar, then the Second Golden Age was the lowest. This does not mean it was a bad time however, after all, it was not referred to as golden for nothing.

After the war against the undead finally came to an end and necromancy was outlawed, punishable by death, there was very little left of the races of mortals. The Samorian race appeared to have become extinct, believed by many to have been wiped out by the swarming undead and turned to its rotten ranks. The population of each surviving race had dwindled to its bare minimum. Fearing extinction themselves, each race began to distrust the others and slowly drifted back to their homelands. Isolating themselves from each other for many, many years.

These small pockets of survivors however, began a hard, yet simple life. There was little to rebuild as the once mighty cities had crumbled to dust under the barrage of necromantic spells. As such, they simply started over. They had no machines left to do the hard work for them, so the people once again grew strong physically as they worked the lands to grow food and built villages for shelter. Over time, these little settlements turned into huge cities but, they were simpler and more functional compared to the cities of the First Golden Age.

Magic became reviled and distrusted by many as they suspected all who wielded the arcane forces to be secret necromancers. Also no technological advances were made during this two hundred year period aside from refining steel producingtechniques. Tools and weapons took time to make as they were hand-forged and so this, along with the dwindling number of people in general, meant that for two centuries, there was no war. No skirmishes between rival villages. No bandits on the roads. No theft. No murder. Despite their lives of long, hard work filled days, the people of Dornat were at piece.

The Second Golden Age ended when Thalgor, the orc-god incited a great anger within his children, encouraging them to wage war against the other races. This lasted many years until Elgun Orlaven, the Orc-Cleaver brought the war to an end.

The Third Golden Age

The Age of Discovery

Upon the defeat of the rampaging tide of orcs that had swept across Dornat, a third age of peace fell upon the world. Very much like the second age, this one was partly caused by the dwindling population after the war with the orcs. Those that survived concentrated their efforts on rebuilding their lives and cities. Dwarven society, which appeared to have taken the least damage to its populace, led the way in this new age. Teams of dwarf engineers spread out across the world to help with the rebuilding, using their engineering skills to speed the building work up.

The dwarves became much revered throughout Dornat at this point, due to their selfless giving and, as such, many other cultures took on aspects of dwarven society. The concept of family-led clans was adopted by smaller human and elvish villages and, dwarven style architecture became the height of fashion. The dwarves, whose proud and self-important nature became inflated by this and were more than happy to respond to such praise by teaching the race of humans their engineering knowledge.

Steam-power became prevalent. Great machines, powered in this way became common-place. Added to this was the “Black-Powder” devices that the dwarves introduced. Cannons and rifles that could kill an enemy at a hundred paces, took the place of bows and swords. The use of steam led to the use of hot air. Once again, ships took to the skies, for the first time in several hundred years. This somewhat rickety mode of transport inspired those with adventurous souls to head into the world and explore. Ancient maps from the First Golden Age stoked the fire of adventure though, many of these maps were forged by those seeking to increase their fortunes at the expense of those they sold them to. As such, many a would-be explorer never returned to their homes but, were honoured as great heroes and pioneers.

It was, of course, a time of peace. This peace only became threatened when the elves objected to the dwarves wish to hew down some of their great forests in order to build a flat, open area of land in which they wished to experiment further with their aviation technology. The dwarves, who had become used to doing more or less as they pleased, due to the high regard they were placed in, neglected to gain permission from the elves, whose land they were in. The elves chose to defend their precious woodlands. But, they revere all life so, first they sent a delegation to the dwarves, to ask them to stop their work, explaining that the trees were sacred to the elves. The dwarves scoffed at their pointy eared allies and stated that the trees were sacred to them also, as they were the fuel of their machines. The elves were outraged and forced the dwarves from their lands at bow-point. It was many, many years before a dwarf was allowed to set foot in Eldoryl again.

Despite this one, minor problem, life during the Third Golden Age was one of great excitement and advancement for the people. Even mages, who had been shunned by many, after the war with the first necromancer, had started to become accepted once more and, the colleges of magic on Sorcerers Island became full of applicants again. Magical knowledge took great strides in those years and many mages worked alongside dwarven engineers to try and recreate the legendary Dragon-Stones that had powered the machines of the first age. Alas, such an endeavour proved fruitless and many gave up the pursuit, believing such knowledge to have been lost forever. (Note: Some orcish shaman had passed down this knowledge by word of mouth since the First Golden Age. The orcs, with their limited intelligence and understanding of the world, were never able to create Dragon-Stones with the same potential as human wizards had in years gone by, but they still had the basic knowledge which modern mages had lost.)

For almost seven hundred years the races of Dornat prospered and flourished. Wars were almost unheard of, except for the occasional skirmish between clans. Yet, even these were little more than drunken arguments that had gotten out of hand. Sadly, as with the previous golden ages, this too came to abrupt end when the arch-devil, Zeniar invaded Dornat. Very quickly, huge portions of the mortal races were obliterated and again, much knowledge was lost. The dwarves retained much of it however, but chose to keep it to themselves, annoyed that the other races no longer held them in such high esteem as they failed to protect the world from Zeniar.
